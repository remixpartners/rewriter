# Pinker: Clarity Principles

Reference for the Strategist. Source: Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014).

---

## Classic Style

The guiding metaphor: seeing the world. The writer sees something the reader hasn't noticed yet, and orients the reader's gaze so she can see it herself.

Two requirements. Show the reader something in the world (concrete or abstract, but there must be something to see). Engage the reader in conversation (the reader is an active participant, not a passive recipient).

Classic style is conversational and visually descriptive. The writer draws attention to the subject, not to themselves. The writer counts on the reader to read between the lines, catch the drift, connect the dots.

What classic style avoids: self-conscious hedging (the academic defense crouch), writing down to the reader ("motherese"), and soggy apologies.

---

## The Curse of Knowledge

"The single best explanation I know of why good people write bad prose."

A difficulty in imagining what it is like for someone else not to know something that you know. Writers underexplain. They include jargon without noticing. They see patterns and abstractions; their readers need examples and concreteness.

How to fight it: recognize it as a handicap. Explain terms instead of assuming shared knowledge. Stage revelations in multiple steps. The more unfamiliar the idea, the more stages required. "Imagining the reader on your shoulder" does not work. You need actual readers or deliberate perspective-taking.

---

## Zombie Nouns

Turning verbs into nouns drains prose of energy. "Decide" becomes "decision." "Fail" becomes "failure." "Analyze" becomes "analysis."

Generator warning sticker A: "Mild Exposure to CO can result in accumulated damage over time."
Generator warning sticker B: "Using a generator indoors CAN KILL YOU IN MINUTES."

Use verbs. Name the actor. Write like the living.

---

## Hedging Kill List

Words that cushion prose with fluff, implying the writer won't stand behind what they say:

almost, apparently, comparatively, fairly, in part, nearly, partially, predominantly, presumably, rather, relatively, seemingly, so to speak, somewhat, sort of, to a certain degree, to some extent, "I would argue"

"You can count on the common sense of readers to fill in the missing hedges and apologies."

---

## Abstraction vs. Concreteness

Abstraction is the luxury of the expert. Novices crave concreteness and cry out for examples. The fix is always the same: replace the abstraction with something the reader can see, hear, or touch.
