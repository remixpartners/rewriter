# Heath: Stickiness Principles

Reference for the Strategist. Source: Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007).

---

## The SUCCESs Framework

For an idea to stick it must make the audience pay attention (UNEXPECTED), understand and remember (CONCRETE), believe (CREDIBLE), care (EMOTIONAL), and act (STORY).

Use this as a diagnostic. When something isn't landing, figure out which principle is missing.

---

## Simple: Find the Core

The military distills every plan into a Commander's Intent: one clear statement of the desired end state. Everything else may change. The intent holds.

Your writing needs a Commander's Intent. What's the one thing? If everything else falls away, what survives?

Good metaphors generate action. Disney calls employees "cast members." You can predict behavior from the metaphor alone. Subway calls employees "sandwich artists." This predicts nothing. A metaphor that doesn't generate predictions is dead.

---

## Unexpected: Break a Pattern

The most basic way to get attention is to break a pattern. Consistent stimulation makes people tune out.

Common sense is the enemy of sticky messages. When they sound like common sense, they float in one ear and out the other. Expose the uncommon parts.

A journalism teacher gave students facts about a faculty trip and asked for a headline. Most rearranged the details. The teacher said: "The lead is: THERE WILL BE NO SCHOOL ON THURSDAY." It's about figuring out why it matters. Students remembered this lesson for decades.

---

## Unexpected: Curiosity Gaps

Tell people just enough for them to realize what's missing from their knowledge. Open gaps before closing them. Our tendency is to deliver facts. First, the reader must realize they need those facts.

Instead of "What information do I need to convey?" ask "What questions do I want my audience to ask?"

---

## Concrete: Make It Tangible

Aesop's ideas never would have lasted as "Aesop's Helpful Suggestions."

"World-class customer service" is abstract. "Nordstrom ironed a customer's shirt from another store" is concrete.

The difference between an expert and a novice is the ability to think abstractly. It's easy to lose awareness that you're talking like an expert.

Memory is like velcro. The more hooks an idea has, the better it clings. Concrete images create more hooks than abstractions.

---

## Credible: The Sinatra Test

If you've played the Palace, you can play anywhere. One compelling proof point settles the question.

A delivery company could say "We are fast and dependable." They say instead: "We handled the release of the Harry Potter book." Enough.

Let people test an idea on themselves. Don't say "people were fooled." Fool them in the telling so they feel it.

---

## Emotional: One Person

"If I look at the mass, I will never act. If I look at the one, I will."

When people think analytically, they stop thinking emotionally. Analysis kills emotion. Focus on the individual case.

People ask "What's in it for my group?" more than "What's in it for me?" Appeal to identity.

Maslow's Basement: we think everyone else is motivated by money and security while we're motivated by meaning. This is why most incentive structures fail.

---

## Story: Simulate

Mental practice produces two-thirds of the benefits of actual physical practice. The right story is a flight simulator for the brain.

Bring people on the journey of mistake, mystery, and discovery. Don't just deliver the outcome. That way they mentally test how they would have handled it.

Arguments invite counter-arguments. Stories engage.
