# Strunk & White: The Elements of Style

Reference for the Craftsman and the Editor. Source: William Strunk Jr. and E. B. White, *The Elements of Style*, 4th Edition (2000).

This is the most important reference in the pipeline. Strunk & White is the backbone of English prose style. The principles here are not suggestions. They are the foundation. When in doubt, follow Strunk.

---

## Core Philosophy

"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that the writer make all sentences short, or avoid all detail and treat subjects only in outline, but that every word tell."

That is the whole book in fifty-nine words. Everything else is elaboration.

## I. Elementary Rules of Usage

These are mechanical. Get them right so the reader never stumbles on grammar when they should be absorbing your point.

### 1. Form the possessive singular by adding 's

Always. Charles's friend. Burns's poems. The witch's malice. Exceptions only for ancient proper names ending in -es or -is: Jesus', Moses' Laws, Isis' temple.

Common error: confusing *it's* (contraction of "it is") with *its* (possessive). "It's a wise dog that scratches its own fleas."

### 2. Use the serial comma

In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, comma after each term except the last. Red, white, and blue. He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents.

Exception: follow the usage of individual business firms (Little, Brown and Company).

### 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas

A parenthetic expression is one that can be lifted from the sentence without destroying its meaning. "The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot."

Key distinction: **restrictive** clauses (no commas, narrows meaning) vs. **nonrestrictive** clauses (commas, adds information).

- Restrictive: People who live in glass houses shouldn't throw stones.
- Nonrestrictive: The audience, which had at first been indifferent, became interested.

Test: can you remove the clause and the sentence still identifies the same subject? If yes, it's nonrestrictive -- use commas.

### 4. Comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause

"The early records of the city have disappeared, and the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed."

When the subject is the same for both clauses and expressed only once, a comma is useful with *but*. With *and*, omit it if the relation is close.

- I have heard the arguments, but am still unconvinced.
- He has had several years' experience and is thoroughly competent.

### 5. Do not join independent clauses with a comma

The comma splice. If two clauses are grammatically complete and not joined by a conjunction, use a semicolon.

- Wrong: Mary Shelley's works are entertaining, they are full of engaging ideas.
- Right: Mary Shelley's works are entertaining; they are full of engaging ideas.
- Also right: Mary Shelley's works are entertaining. They are full of engaging ideas.
- Also right: Mary Shelley's works are entertaining, for they are full of engaging ideas.

Exception: very short, parallel clauses. "Man proposes, God disposes." "I hardly knew him, he was so changed."

### 6. Do not break sentences in two

Do not use periods where commas belong.

- Wrong: I met them on a Cunard liner. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.
- Right: I met them on a Cunard liner, coming home from Liverpool to New York.

Exception: deliberate emphasis. "Again and again he called out. No reply."

### 7. Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, an appositive, an amplification, or a quotation

The colon says: what follows is closely related to what came before. It should follow an independent clause -- do not separate a verb from its complement with a colon.

- Wrong: Your whittler requires: a knife, a piece of wood, and a back porch.
- Right: Your whittler requires three props: a knife, a piece of wood, and a back porch.

Join two independent clauses with a colon if the second interprets or amplifies the first.

### 8. Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or interruption

A dash is stronger than a comma, less formal than a colon, more relaxed than parentheses. Use it only when a more common mark seems inadequate.

- His first thought on getting out of bed -- if he had any thought at all -- was to get back in again.

Use a dash sparingly. When everything is emphasized, nothing is.

### 9. The number of the subject determines the number of the verb

Words between subject and verb do not affect the verb's number. "The bittersweet flavor of youth -- its trials, its joys, its adventures, its challenges -- is not soon forgotten."

Use singular verbs after *each*, *either*, *everyone*, *everybody*, *neither*, *nobody*, *someone*.

A compound subject with *and* takes a plural verb. But some compounds are so inseparable they take a singular: "Bread and butter was all she served."

### 10. Use the proper case of pronoun

Use the simple personal pronoun as a subject. "Blake and I stayed home" (not "Blake and myself"). Use the simple personal pronoun as an object. "Let's talk it over between us, then, you and me."

Gerunds require the possessive: "Mother objected to our driving on the icy roads."

### 11. A participial phrase at the beginning must refer to the grammatical subject

- Wrong: Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap.
- Right: Because the house was in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy it cheap.

Sentences violating this rule are often ludicrous: "Wondering what to do next, the clock struck twelve."

## II. Elementary Principles of Composition

These are the rules that matter most to the Craftsman. They govern how sentences become paragraphs and paragraphs become prose.

### 12. Choose a suitable design and hold to it

Planning must be a deliberate prelude to writing. The more clearly the writer perceives the shape of what is to come and pursues that shape, the better the chances of success.

### 13. Make the paragraph the unit of composition

Each paragraph deals with a single topic. The beginning of each paragraph signals a new step in the development of the subject.

Begin each paragraph with a sentence that suggests the topic or helps the transition. In narration, the paragraph sometimes begins with a concise, comprehensive statement holding together the details that follow.

Break long paragraphs for visual relief. But don't fire off many short paragraphs in quick succession -- that reads like advertising copy. Moderation and a sense of order.

### 14. Use the active voice

The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive.

- Passive: My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.
- Active: I shall always remember my first visit to Boston.

When a sentence is made stronger, it usually becomes shorter. Brevity is a by-product of vigor.

Replace perfunctory expressions like *there is* and *could be heard* with transitive verbs in the active voice:

| Passive / Weak | Active / Strong |
|---|---|
| There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground. | Dead leaves covered the ground. |
| At dawn the crowing of a rooster could be heard. | The cock's crow came with dawn. |
| The reason he left college was that his health became impaired. | Failing health compelled him to leave college. |
| It was not long before she was very sorry that she had said what she had. | She soon repented her words. |

The passive is acceptable when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when the subject of the paragraph dictates it. "The dramatists of the Restoration are little esteemed today" is preferable in a paragraph about Restoration dramatists. Use judgment.

### 15. Put statements in positive form

Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, noncommittal language. Use *not* as a means of denial or antithesis, never as a means of evasion.

| Negative / Evasive | Positive / Direct |
|---|---|
| He was not very often on time. | He usually came late. |
| She did not think studying Latin was sensible. | She thought the study of Latin a waste of time. |
| not honest | dishonest |
| did not remember | forgot |
| did not pay any attention to | ignored |
| did not have much confidence in | distrusted |

If your every sentence admits a doubt with *would*, *should*, *could*, *may*, *might*, and *can*, your writing will lack authority. Save the auxiliaries for situations involving real uncertainty.

### 16. Use definite, specific, concrete language

Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.

| Vague | Concrete |
|---|---|
| A period of unfavorable weather set in. | It rained every day for a week. |
| He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward. | He grinned as he pocketed the coin. |

The greatest writers -- Homer, Dante, Shakespeare -- are effective because they deal in particulars and report details that matter. Their words call up pictures.

Orwell's illustration: the passage from Ecclesiastes ("I returned, and saw under the sun, that the race is not to the swift...") versus its abstract translation ("Objective consideration of contemporary phenomena compels the conclusion that success exhibits no tendency to be commensurate with innate capacity..."). Both say the same thing. One lives. One is dead.

### 17. Omit needless words

The cardinal rule. Every word must earn its place.

| Wordy | Tight |
|---|---|
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt / doubtless |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject that | this subject |
| the reason why is that | because |

**"The fact that" is an especially debilitating expression.** Revise it out of every sentence.

| Kill | Replace with |
|---|---|
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | though / although |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you / notify you |
| I was unaware of the fact that | I was unaware that / I did not know |

*Who is, which was* and the like are often superfluous:
- His cousin, who is a member of the same firm --> His cousin, a member of the same firm
- Trafalgar, which was Nelson's last battle --> Trafalgar, Nelson's last battle

A common way to fall into wordiness: presenting a single complex idea step by step in a series of sentences that could be combined into one. Macbeth's ambition in 51 words vs. 26 words.

### 18. Avoid a succession of loose sentences

Loose sentences have two clauses, the second introduced by a conjunction or relative. One is fine. A whole paragraph of them creates mechanical, singsong prose.

Fix: replace some with simple sentences, semicoloned sentences, or periodic sentences. Vary the structure to represent the real relations of thought.

Compare the singsong concert review ("The third concert was given last evening, and a large audience was in attendance...") with Forster's varied, alive prose in "What I Believe."

### 19. Express coordinate ideas in similar form

Parallel construction. The likeness of form enables the reader to recognize the likeness of content.

- Wrong: Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed.
- Right: Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method.

An article or preposition applying to all members of a series must either appear before the first term only or be repeated before each term.

- Wrong: the French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese
- Right: the French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese

Correlative expressions (*both...and*, *not only...but also*, *either...or*, *first...second...third*) must be followed by the same grammatical construction.

- Wrong: It was both a long ceremony and very tedious.
- Right: The ceremony was both long and tedious.

### 20. Keep related words together

The position of words in a sentence is the principal means of showing their relationship. Confusion and ambiguity result when words are badly placed.

- Ambiguous: He noticed a large stain in the rug that was right in the center.
- Clear: He noticed a large stain right in the center of the rug.

The subject and principal verb should not, as a rule, be separated by a phrase or clause that can be transferred to the beginning.

- Interrupted: Toni Morrison, in *Beloved*, writes about characters who have escaped from slavery.
- Better: In *Beloved*, Toni Morrison writes about characters who have escaped from slavery.

The relative pronoun should come immediately after its antecedent.

Modifiers should come, if possible, next to the words they modify.

- Wrong: She only found two mistakes.
- Right: She found only two mistakes.

### 21. In summaries, keep to one tense

Don't shift between past and present. Shifting gives the appearance of uncertainty and irresolution.

### 22. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end

The proper place for the word or group of words the writer desires to make most prominent is usually the end. The other prominent position is the beginning.

- Weak ending: Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways.
- Strong ending: Since that time, humanity has advanced in many ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude.

The principle applies equally to sentences within a paragraph and paragraphs within a composition.

## III. Matters of Form (Selected)

**Colloquialisms.** If you use slang, use it. Don't put it in quotation marks as if apologizing.

**Exclamations.** Don't emphasize simple statements with exclamation points. "It was a wonderful show." Not "It was a wonderful show!" Reserve exclamation marks for true exclamations and commands.

**Parentheses.** The sentence containing a parenthetical expression is punctuated outside the last mark of parenthesis exactly as if the parenthetical expression were absent.

**Quotations.** Formal quotations are introduced by a colon. Quotations in apposition or as direct objects take a comma. Quotations introduced by *that* are indirect discourse -- no quotation marks.

## IV. Words and Expressions to Kill

These words are not wrong English. They are bad style -- the commonplaces of careless writing. Many can be caught by search-and-replace.

### Bankrupt Words (replace or delete)

| Word | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| case | Often unnecessary | "In many cases, the rooms lacked AC" --> "Many rooms lacked AC" |
| certainly | Indiscriminate intensifier | Delete or replace with evidence |
| character | Redundant padding | "acts of a hostile character" --> "hostile acts" |
| clever | Overused, means "good-natured" not "ingenious" | Choose a precise word |
| contact (verb) | Vague and self-important | Get in touch with, phone, find, meet |
| currently | Usually redundant with present tense | Delete or use a specific time |
| effect (noun) | Vague in arts writing | Name the specific quality |
| facility | Euphemism | Call the prison a prison, the school a school |
| factor | Hackneyed | "She won the match by being better trained" |
| feature | Adds nothing | Tell what was featured instead |
| finalize | Pompous, ambiguous | Complete, conclude, put in final form |
| fix | Colloquial; prefer *arrange*, *prepare*, *mend* for formal writing | Context-dependent |
| important(ly) | As sentence adverb, avoid | "What's more" or rephrase |
| in terms of | Padding | Delete: "unattractive in terms of salary" --> "the salary made the job unattractive" |
| insightful | Suspicious overstatement | Perceptive, or just describe the insight |
| interesting | Unconvincing announcement | Make it interesting instead of saying so |
| kind of / sort of | Restrict to literal sense | "rather" or "something like" for the hedge meaning |
| meaningful | Bankrupt adjective | "His contribution counted heavily" |
| most | Not a substitute for *almost* | Use *almost* |
| nature | Redundant like *character* | "hostile nature" --> delete |
| nice | Shaggy, all-purpose | Reserve for "precise" or "delicate" |
| offputting, ongoing | Inexact, clumsy | Objectionable, continuing, active |
| one of the most | Feeble formula, threadbare | Rephrase |
| -oriented | Clumsy pretentious suffix | "a company chiefly concerned with manufacturing" |
| personalize | Pretentious | "make it good and keep it clean" |
| possess | Pompous for *have* or *own* | She had great courage (not "possessed") |
| prestigious | Adjective of last resort | Find a reason for the prestige |
| thrust | Showy, darling of executives | The point, the purpose |
| transpire | Does not mean "happen" | Use *happen*, *occur*, *come to pass* |
| type | Not a synonym for *kind of* | "that kind of employee" |
| unique | Means "without like or equal" -- no degrees | Not "most unique" or "very unique" |
| utilize | Prefer *use* | Always |
| very | Use sparingly | Use words strong in themselves |
| worthwhile | Overworked, emaciated | Promising, useful, valuable, exciting |

### Expressions to Revise

| Expression | Fix |
|---|---|
| and/or | Choose one; or say "X or Y or both" |
| as to whether | *Whether* is sufficient |
| as yet | *Yet* is sufficient |
| the fact that | Delete (see Rule 17) |
| the foreseeable future | Cliche. How far is foreseeable? |
| in the last analysis | Bankrupt. Delete. |
| the truth is / the fact is | Don't give the truth advance billing. State it. |
| thanking you in advance | Presumptuous. "Will you please" or "I shall be obliged" |

### Commonly Confused Pairs (Still Useful)

These distinctions remain genuinely useful in business writing. Pairs where modern usage has settled the question (singular "they," "hopefully" as sentence adverb, "data" as singular) have been removed -- follow current standard usage for those.

| Pair | Distinction |
|---|---|
| affect / effect | Affect (verb): influence. Effect (noun): result. Effect (verb): accomplish. |
| comprise / constitute | A zoo comprises animals. Animals constitute a zoo. Never "comprised of." |
| disinterested / uninterested | Impartial vs. not interested |
| fewer / less | Fewer: number (countable). Less: quantity (uncountable). |
| imply / infer | Speaker implies; listener infers |
| that / which | That: defining/restrictive (no comma). Which: nondefining/nonrestrictive (comma). |

## V. An Approach to Style (The 21 Reminders)

These are not rules. They are reminders -- "they state what most of us know and at times forget." They are White's contribution: attitudes more than mechanics, the difference between correct writing and good writing.

### 1. Place yourself in the background

Draw the reader's attention to the sense and substance of the writing, not to the mood and temper of the author. To achieve style, begin by affecting none. A careful and honest writer does not need to worry about style.

### 2. Write in a way that comes naturally

Use words and phrases that come readily to hand. But do not assume that because you have acted naturally, your product is without flaw. Natural writing still requires revision.

### 3. Work from a suitable design

Design informs even the simplest structure. "Columbus didn't just sail, he sailed west." Plan the shape before you write. But if emotion overrides design, let it -- you'll probably have to reorganize anyway.

### 4. Write with nouns and verbs

Not with adjectives and adverbs. "The adjective hasn't been built that can pull a weak or inaccurate noun out of a tight place." Nouns and verbs give good writing its toughness and color.

### 5. Revise and rewrite

Few writers produce what they are after on the first try. Do not be afraid to experiment. It is no sign of weakness that your manuscript ends up in need of major surgery.

### 6. Do not overwrite

Rich, ornate prose is hard to digest, generally unwholesome, and sometimes nauseating. Guard against wordiness. Reread your writing and ruthlessly delete the excess.

### 7. Do not overstate

A single overstatement, wherever it occurs, diminishes the whole. A single carefree superlative can destroy, for readers, the object of your enthusiasm. Readers go instantly on guard.

### 8. Avoid the use of qualifiers

*Rather, very, little, pretty* -- "these are the leeches that infest the pond of prose, sucking the blood of words." Use words strong in themselves.

### 9. Do not affect a breezy manner

The breezy style is the work of an egocentric who imagines that everything that comes to mind is of general interest. Spontaneity is not genius. Compare the breezy writer trying hard to be fun with the compact, informative, unpretentious writer who dug up a real item of news and presented it straight.

### 10. Use orthodox spelling

Do not write *nite*, *thru*, *pleez*. These distract the reader and exhaust patience.

### 11. Do not explain too much

Be sparing with adverbs after "he said" and "she replied." Let the conversation itself disclose the speaker's manner.

### 12. Do not construct awkward adverbs

Do not write *tangledly* or *tiredly*. If the adverb is not used orally, do not put it on paper. Do not dress words up by adding -ly as though putting a hat on a horse.

### 13. Make sure the reader knows who is speaking

Dialogue is a total loss unless you indicate who the speaker is. Obscurity is an imposition on the reader.

### 14. Avoid fancy words

Avoid the elaborate, the pretentious, the coy, and the cute. Anglo-Saxon is a livelier tongue than Latin, so use Anglo-Saxon words. Do not be tempted by a twenty-dollar word when there is a ten-center handy, ready, and able.

The line between the fancy and the plain, between the atrocious and the felicitous, is sometimes alarmingly fine. The question of ear is vital. Cock your ear.

### 15. Do not use dialect unless your ear is good

If you use dialect, be consistent. The best dialect writers are economical -- they use the minimum, not the maximum, of deviation from the norm.

### 16. Be clear

Clarity, clarity, clarity. Since writing is communication, clarity comes closest to being the prize. When a sentence is hopelessly mired, start fresh. Usually what is wrong is that the construction has become too involved at some point; the sentence needs to be broken apart.

"Muddiness is not merely a disturber of prose, it is also a destroyer of life, of hope."

### 17. Do not inject opinion

Unless there is a good reason for its being there, do not inject opinion into a piece of writing. Opinions scattered indiscriminately leave the mark of egotism on a work.

### 18. Use figures of speech sparingly

Similes are useful, but in rapid fire they are more distracting than illuminating. Do not mix metaphors. "Don't start by calling something a swordfish and end by calling it an hourglass."

### 19. Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity

Write out names of organizations instead of using initials unless you are certain the reader will know them. Start with the full name; shorten later when the reader has gotten bearings.

### 20. Avoid foreign languages

Write in English. Some writers sprinkle foreign expressions from sheer exuberance or a desire to show off, with no regard for the reader's comfort. It is a bad habit.

### 21. Prefer the standard to the offbeat

In choosing between the formal and the informal, the regular and the offbeat, the general and the special, the beginner should err on the side of conservatism. No idiom is taboo, no accent forbidden; there is simply a better chance of doing well if the writer holds a steady course, enters the stream of English quietly, and does not thrash about.

Business has constructed a language of its own. "Its portentous nouns and verbs invest ordinary events with high adventure; executives walk among toner cartridges, caparisoned like knights." The question is whether business vocabulary is helpful to ordinary prose. Usually, the same ideas can be expressed less formidably, if one makes the effort.

*Finalize* is special, not standard -- "a peculiarly fuzzy and silly word." Prefer the standard.

## How to Use This Reference

**For the Craftsman:** Consult Principles 14-17 and 22 on every pass. Active voice (14), positive form (15), concrete language (16), omit needless words (17), and emphatic endings (22) are the five moves that fix 80% of weak prose. The Reminders from Chapter V -- especially 4 (nouns and verbs), 6 (do not overwrite), 7 (do not overstate), 8 (avoid qualifiers), and 14 (avoid fancy words) -- are your quality filters.

**For the Scrub Agent:** The "Words and Expressions to Kill" section overlaps with and reinforces the anti-AI checklist. LLMs are repeat offenders on: *utilize*, *facilitate*, *leverage*, *factor*, *currently*, *in terms of*, *the fact that*, *meaningful*, *insightful*, *innovative*, and every word on the bankrupt list. Cross-reference both lists.

**For the Editor:** Principles 18 (vary sentence structure), 19 (parallel construction), and 20 (keep related words together) are your coherence checks during the final pass. Reminder 16 (be clear) is the final standard: if a reader might misunderstand it, fix it.
