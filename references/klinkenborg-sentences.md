# Klinkenborg: Sentence Craft

Reference for the Craftsman. Source: Verlyn Klinkenborg, *Several Short Sentences About Writing* (2012). Supplemented by Strunk & White's *The Elements of Style*.

---

## The Sentence Is the Unit

Your job as a writer is making sentences. Your other jobs include fixing sentences, killing sentences, and arranging sentences.

The only link between you and the reader is the sentence you're making. There's no sign of your intention apart from the sentences themselves.

Don't overlook the shape of the sentence to get to the meaning. Your meaning depends entirely on the sentence you're making.

Good writing is significant everywhere. Writing isn't about getting the reader to "the point" at the end. Most sentences you make will need to be killed. The rest will need to be fixed.

---

## Short by Default

Long sentences collapse, break down, become opaque, trip over their own awkwardness. The end of a long sentence commonly forgets its beginning.

Strong lengthy sentences are really just strong short sentences joined in various ways.

A crowded sentence means the writer is worried the reader won't follow if parted by a period.

Keep the space between sentences empty. Most sentences need no preamble and no postlude.

---

## Kill Transitions

Forget transitions. You can get anywhere from anywhere, almost instantly.

Why were you taught to dwell on transitions? It was assumed you can't write clearly. That the reader needs a handrail through your prose. Are you that kind of reader? Do you tumble through the gaps between paragraphs? Do you require constant supervision?

No sentence can afford to be merely transitional. If you've written clearly, the reader will follow without them.

---

## Kill Logical Indicators

"On the other hand." "Therefore." "Whereas." "Thus."

These insist upon logic whether it exists or not. They come first in the sentence, trying to steer from the front, as if the reader were incapable of following a shift mid-sentence.

If a piece is assured in its order, it needs no logical indicators.

Try removing "but" wherever you can. The sense of reversal is usually still present without it.

---

## Reject Automatic Sentences

A cliché isn't just a familiar saying. It's the debris of someone else's thinking.

Sentences often volunteer a shape and supply their own words. Those sentences are nearly always dull and unvarying, yielding only the most predictable phrases.

Only revision tells you whether a sentence that offers itself is worth keeping.

All the idea of inspiration will do is stop you from revising a bad sentence.

---

## Vary Rhythm

The enemy is uniformity. A short sentence after a long one lands with weight. A long sentence after several short ones opens breathing room.

Read aloud. The ear is smarter than the eye. It's slower, and it can hear rhythm and unwanted repetition. Read until your ear detects a disturbance. Stop there.

---

## Revise by Deletion

The simplest revision is deletion. There's often a fine sentence lurking within a bad one, a better sentence hiding under a good one.

You can almost never fix a sentence using only the words it already contains.

Remove words instead of adding them.

---

## On Logic and Persuasion

Don't assume logic persuades the reader instead of clarity.

There's little actual logic in good writing. There's a current of thoughts and ideas and observations.

Writing rarely persuades. It does something better. It attests. It suggests the possibilities of the world around you.

---

## On the Reader

Authority rests with the reader, who can simply stop reading.

The question isn't whether the reader can follow you (grammar). The question is whether the reader will follow you (desire).

Trusting the reader controls the temptation to over-describe and over-interpret. It lets the reader share the burden of comprehension.

Read your own work as a reader.

---

## Strunk & White Essentials

Omit needless words. Use the active voice. Put statements in positive form. Use definite, specific, concrete language. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.
