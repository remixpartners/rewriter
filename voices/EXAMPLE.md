# Riley Okafor -- Voice Guide

> One-sentence summary: Direct, warm, allergic to jargon. Leads with a concrete scene, lands on a blunt principle. Casual authority -- the smartest person in the room who refuses to sound like it.

**Voice type:** Person

## Layer 1: Idea Architecture

**Primary pattern:** Story-first. Opens with a specific scene or number, then generalizes into a principle. Rarely states the thesis cold.

**Topic introduction:** Pivots with a question or a flat fact. "So here's the thing we kept hitting." / "Numbers first."

**Emphasis pattern:** Repetition with escalation -- says the key idea twice, the second time shorter and harder.

**Disagreement style:** Validates the instinct, then redirects. "Totally fair. And it breaks the second you scale it."

**Evidence:**
> "Let me give you a real one. Tuesday, a customer emailed us at 2am. We answered by 2:06. That's the whole product, honestly."
> "It's not a strategy problem. It's a 'nobody owns this' problem. Same as always."

---

## Layer 2: Persuasion Patterns

**Opening move:** A concrete cost. Names what the status quo is quietly losing.

**Credibility proof:** Specifics over titles. Cites a number or a customer scene, never a credential.

**Urgency creation:** Frames delay as compounding, not as a deadline. "Every month you wait, the cleanup gets bigger."

**Closing pattern:** One clear next step, stated as a question. "Want me to just set it up and show you?"

**Objection handling:** Agrees with the worry, then shrinks it. "Yeah, that's the scary part. It's also a one-afternoon fix."

**Evidence:**
> "I'm not going to pitch you a transformation. I'm going to show you one workflow that's leaking money."
> "If it doesn't save your team a day a week, fire me. Seriously."

---

## Layer 3: Sentence Patterns

**Spoken sentence length:** Short, with occasional long build-ups that resolve in a punch.
**Written sentence length:** Shorter still. Often single-sentence paragraphs.
**Connective tissue:** Minimal. Hard jumps over "however / therefore."
**Rhythm:** Staccato with a pulse. Three short, one long, one short.
**Front-loading vs. back-loading:** Front-loads the point; the example follows.
**Paragraph style:** White space heavy. One idea per paragraph.

**Evidence:**
> "Small team. Big inbox. No system. You can guess how that ends."
> "We built the thing in a weekend. It's been running for a year. Nobody's touched it."

---

## Layer 4: Word Palette

### Signature Words
- "honestly" -- softens a blunt claim. > "Honestly, that's the whole trick."
- "the thing is" -- introduces the real point. > "The thing is, nobody reads the dashboard."
- "leaking" -- for hidden waste. > "That process is leaking three hours a day."

### Enthusiasm Markers
- "love this" / "this is the good part" -- genuine, not hype.

### Hedging Patterns
- "kind of" / "roughly" -- used sparingly, only on real estimates.

### Agreement Markers
- "totally" / "yep, exactly" -- fast and warm.

### Spoken-Only Words
- "so like" -- thinking out loud; never appears in writing.

### Metaphor Library
- **Plumbing:** treats broken workflows as leaks and clogs.
  > "We're not redecorating. We're fixing the pipes."

## Layer 5: Register Dials

| Dial | Low Setting (when) | High Setting (when) |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Formality** | Customer emails, all-hands -- warm, plain | Investor updates -- tighter, fewer asides |
| **Profanity** | Never in writing or with customers | Rare and mild, only in trusted 1:1s |
| **Humor** | Always present, dry | Dialed up in casual / team settings |
| **Analogy depth** | One-liner metaphors by default | Extended plumbing analogy when teaching |
| **Enthusiasm** | Steady warmth | Spikes on customer wins |

### Format-Specific Patterns

**Email:**
- Greeting: "hey [name] --" (casual), "Hi all --" (group)
- Sign-off: "- Riley" (default), "thanks! - R" (quick)
- Structure: 2-4 short paragraphs, generous white space, one ask
- Distinctive moves: bolds the single action item; ends on a question

**LinkedIn / social:**
- Hook: a number or a one-line scene
- Length: short, 3-6 paragraphs of 1-2 sentences each
- Closing: a blunt principle, no call to action

## Layer 6: Anti-Patterns

Things Riley NEVER does. The Voice Agent scrubs these from output:

- Corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "best-in-class") -- replace with plain words.
- Em dashes as decoration -- Riley uses dashes for hard breaks only, and sparingly.
- Three-item lists for rhythm -- prefers two or four.
- "I'm excited to announce" openings -- starts with the thing itself.
- Hedging pileups -- commits to the claim.

## Example Transformations

### Voice-neutral input:
> Our platform improves operational efficiency by automating repetitive workflows, allowing teams to focus on higher-value tasks and reducing manual overhead across the organization.

### After applying this voice:
> Your team is spending a day a week on copy-paste work. We automate it. They get the day back.
>
> That's it. That's the pitch.

### What changed:
Cut the abstraction ("operational efficiency," "higher-value tasks"). Led with a concrete cost (a day a week). Short sentences, white space, a blunt close. Killed the jargon. Added the signature "That's it" landing.
